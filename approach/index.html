The situation we work with

Relocation is not a logistics problem

Relocation rarely becomes difficult where people expect it to. In most cases, friction does not come from administration, housing, or formal processes, but from implicit cultural rules – the things nobody explains, yet everyone silently assumes.

What newcomers often experience as “something feels off” is not a personal failure, nor a lack of competence. It is an interpretive gap between two cultural logics. Signals are read differently, reactions are weighted differently, and intentions do not always land where they were meant to.

This training does not pathologise these situations. It treats them as normal, structurally understandable moments that emerge whenever people move between cultural systems. The goal is not to eliminate difference, but to make it legible.

Our approach is not instructional and not prescriptive. We work with precise information, but always from the assumption that participants are already capable professionals – operating with a different cultural code. The task is not correction, but orientation.

What we mean by culture

Culture is not only customs, but patterns

In this training, culture is clearly separated from etiquette lists and surface-level “do’s and don’ts”. Culture is not treated as a collection of habits, but as a system of patterns that shape how people interpret situations and respond to one another.

  • how rules are perceived and applied
  • how disagreement and conflict are signalled and managed
  • how authority and hierarchy are approached
  • how directness, distance, and familiarity are calibrated

Hungarian culture, in this sense, is not accidental. It functions as an internally coherent system shaped by historical layers, collective experiences, and a specific cultural self-image.

These dimensions are present throughout the training, but not as abstract theory. They appear as context – background logic that helps participants understand why certain reactions, behaviours, or communication styles feel unexpected, disproportionate, or ambiguous in everyday and professional situations.

The focus is not on learning cultural facts, but on recognising recurring patterns that make present-day interactions intelligible.
