Cultural empathy as a skill

Adaptation is not imitation

Cultural empathy is often misunderstood as a matter of attitude or goodwill. In reality, it functions much more like a situational skill – something that can be learned, refined, and applied with increasing precision.

In the context of relocation and cross-cultural work, empathy does not mean accepting everything, blending in uncritically, or attempting to “become local”. Imitation is rarely authentic, and it is rarely sustainable.

What tends to work instead is the ability to read situations accurately and respond in proportion to what is actually taking place.

  • sensitivity to contextual signals
  • a sense of proportion in response
  • the ability to choose situation-specific reactions instead of relying on reflexes

Rather than rehearsing ideal behaviours, the focus is on developing interpretive capacity – recognising what kind of interaction one is in, and what kind of response the situation actually calls for.

This becomes especially important in moments that appear tense or ambiguous. In many cultural contexts, including Hungary, not every signal of tension indicates a real conflict. Smoke is not fire.

Positions are often signalled before they are stated directly, and disagreement may be communicated indirectly long before it becomes explicit.

Effective responses in such situations are rarely a matter of assertiveness or restraint alone. They are a matter of precision – understanding when to respond, how strongly, and in what register.

Cultural empathy, in this sense, is not about adapting one’s identity. It is about maintaining agency while navigating unfamiliar cultural logics with clarity, proportion, and confidence.

This perspective forms the basis of our cross-cultural training programmes for international professionals and organisations operating in Hungary.
